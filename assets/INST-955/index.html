Discipline-Specific Topics in Instructional Technology

INST-955

Professor Laurie Keating

Spring 2013

Reflection

This class more than any other provoked a lot of thought about education and its direction over the next 5, 10, 20 years. As a class we explored new trends in the education space like the Flipped Classroom, hardware initiatives like BYOD and 1:1 programs, and the need for districts to evaluate new teachers for technology competency before hiring so that new teachers are ready to use and implement the new technology intiatives as they begin. Each of these changes are either already happening in districts, or are on the horizon. Similar to other classes, this class encouraged discussion via the discussion board and it was often lively, but more importantly, informative.

I brought to the class the outside perspective of a corporate training environment. I was most interested in understanding some of the similarities and differences between the two environments. Instructional technology can fit into any educational setting. It is certainly not limited to education. It was great to be able to share my more corporate experience with the class. Many of them were teachers and had never worked in an office, or a large corporation. So offering that perspective and talking about some of the instructional methods always created a great dialog. In the same way, listening to teachers talk about their strategies and methods for using technology in their classrooms allowed me to broaden my approach to instructional technology at work.

In my final project I chose to do an instructional video about an experimental product that I was working with a team at McGraw-Hill Education to develop. It was a product that I would eventually need to develop a training program for, and so the assignment of writing out an entire lesson plan allowed me to think about the training much more structurally. Having gained the skills for lesson planning in other courses, this was fairly easy. However, in this case it was a practical lesson that I would actually be able to use at work. The most useful part of the lesson was exploring the NETS*T standards and being able to teach to a framework designed specifically for adult learning. This is a resource I have used since this project in developing training programs. Those standards help to focus my training sessions on practical and valuable skills.
