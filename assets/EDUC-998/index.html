Language Development and Communication

EDUC-998

Professor Loy Riley

Spring 2013

Reflection

This course was one of the most challenging for me. Part of the reason for that was that it had very little application to my experience or situation. While it was helpful to learn about language development, I will never be responsible in my line of work for working with children to properly develop their speech skills, and work through issues as they grow up. I was able to gain some information on how to work with adults with speech problems. The class definitely helped me understand how to approach colleagues or trainees that had difficulty with their speech. In that sense, the course was valuable, but since its focus was mainly on language development I was working with people who had gone through all of the phases we studied.

The field experience for this course was a good experience for me even though it was not related to my current line of work. I worked with a speech/language pathologist by observing and working with some of the students she was responsible for. Of particular interest was her work with a young hispanic boy. Having grown up learning French, I've always been fascinated with being bilingual. I really enjoyed watching the speech pathologist work with the young student on developing proper English sounds and pronunciations. I don't remember much of my early experience learning French in school, but watching this young boy struggle to learn the English language was very helpful in providing insight into what I experienced.

Of course, the other benefit of watching the speech pathologist work with the young student was the ability to understand the struggle of some of the people I work with from all over the world. In studying language development over the course of the class, it was clear that language development, particularly new language development is much easier as a child than an adult. This concept, as well as observing the young student's difficulty proved helpful to me in developing an understanding and patience for those I'm training who are native language speakers.
