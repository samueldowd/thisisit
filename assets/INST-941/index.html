Internet for 21st Century Teaching and Learning

INST-941

Professor Don Langenhorst

Spring 2013

Reflection

This course was among the more fascinating of all of the Educational Technology courses that I took as part of this program. While the course covered a breadth of topics, it also offered a fair amount of depth into each one.

In each of the assignments listed below, we were asked to browse the Web and find specific resources that supported the text we were reading. This was very helpful for two reasons. One, it reinforced what we had read in the text with real-world examples. Two, it provided us with several great resources for things like research, web site evaluation, and search engines. Again, all of these resources were applicable not only in the course but in many aspects of my work with educational technology.

The discussion posts were engaging and frequently very active. We covered many topics related to using the Internet to supplement teaching and learning in the 21st century. Among the most helpful part of the discussions was the ability to learn from my peers. Since I am not a teacher in a school district it was invaluable to hear from school teachers about their experiences with educational technology in an actual classroom. The live chats which we had several times over the course of the semester were also very informative as well as engaging. They often generated passionate debate and commentary. For an online course this allowed for a lot of great conversation and even relationship building which led to great professional connections.

My favorite part of the course was the projects. In the group project I worked with my team to build a cross-curricular Webquest for High School students that was based on the book "To Kill a Mockingbird" but also incorporated the study of the Jim Crow trials. This project broadened my ability to plan lessons and come up with the right synergy between pedagogy and technology. In addition, this group project taught me a lot about formative assessment, which is now something I use even in my professional life, albeit more informally, to gauge the understanding of people I am training or explaining products to.

The final project involved designing a Webquest that I could use with my colleagues at Harvard Businesss School to explain the new Cloud Storage System that we were implementing. The Webquest allowed participants to explore the options available for a Cloud Storage Solution, and prepare a presentation to the executive committee with their recommendation. This project helped me by developing a sense of how to create projects for groups that allowed participants to contribute with their own individual skills and talents. This is a tool I now use when working or forming committees or other working groups at work.
